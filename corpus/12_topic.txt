Source: Research Corpus

Topic: AI Ethics, Bias, and Fairness

As AI systems are deployed in high-stakes domains, ensuring they are fair, unbiased, and ethical has become critical.

Types of AI Bias:
- Historical bias: Training data reflects past societal inequalities
- Representation bias: Underrepresentation of certain groups in training data
- Measurement bias: Proxies used for sensitive attributes introduce errors
- Aggregation bias: One model for diverse populations ignores subgroup differences
- Deployment bias: Model used in context different from training context

Fairness Definitions (often mutually exclusive):
- Demographic parity: Equal positive prediction rates across groups
- Equalized odds: Equal true positive and false positive rates across groups
- Individual fairness: Similar individuals should be treated similarly
- Counterfactual fairness: Outcome same in counterfactual where sensitive attribute differs

Bias Detection Tools:
- IBM AI Fairness 360: Comprehensive bias detection and mitigation library
- Microsoft Fairlearn: Fairness assessment and mitigation for scikit-learn
- Google What-If Tool: Visual exploration of ML model behavior

Mitigation Strategies:
Pre-processing: Reweigh training data, augment underrepresented groups
In-processing: Add fairness constraints to training objective
Post-processing: Adjust decision thresholds per group

LLM-Specific Concerns:
- Stereotyping: LLMs can produce biased text about demographic groups
- Toxicity: Models can generate harmful, offensive content
- Privacy: LLMs may memorize and reproduce training data (PII leakage)
- Disinformation: LLMs can generate convincing false content at scale

Regulatory Landscape:
EU AI Act (2024): Risk-based framework. High-risk AI (hiring, lending, medical) requires conformity assessment, transparency, and human oversight. Prohibited uses include real-time biometric surveillance.
